# BRAINLIFT — Learning-Science Source Facts & Summaries

A primary-source fact sheet distilled from the **Resource library (Section 3) of
[`LEARNING_SCIENCE.md`](./LEARNING_SCIENCE.md)**. For each paper/article it gives a
plain-language **Summary** and a list of concrete **Key facts** — effect sizes,
sample sizes, study designs, dates, and the caveats/limitations the authors
themselves note. The final **Master fact sheet** is a single deduplicated,
scannable list of the most usable numbers across all sources.

> **How to use this.** Skim a theme to refresh the evidence behind a learning
> principle, or jump to the Master fact sheet for a quick, citable number. Figures
> are taken from the primary text wherever possible; where a source was paywalled,
> scanned, or blocked, the fact is flagged and the fallback noted (see the
> [Access & limitations](#access--limitations) section at the end). Where a
> commonly repeated figure turned out to be mis-attributed or unverifiable, that
> is called out explicitly rather than repeated.

**Conventions.** *d* = Cohen's d / Hedges' g (standardized mean difference, ~0.2
small / 0.5 medium / 0.8 large); *SD* / *sigma* = standard deviation; *r* =
correlation; *g* used where the source reports Hedges' g; "pp" = percentage
points. "Secondary" means the number came from a reputable summary/mirror rather
than the primary full text.

## 1. Foundations / "what works" overviews

### Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan & Willingham (2013) — *Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques*

**Citation.** Dunlosky, J., Rawson, K. A., Marsh, E. J., Nathan, M. J., & Willingham, D. T. (2013). "Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques: Promising Directions From Cognitive and Educational Psychology." *Psychological Science in the Public Interest, 14*(1), 4–58.
**URL.** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1529100612453266 · free practitioner summary (Dunlosky, "Strengthening the Student Toolbox," *American Educator* 2013): https://www.aft.org/sites/default/files/media/2014/dunlosky.pdf

**Summary.** A monograph reviewing 10 widely usable study techniques and rating each "high / moderate / low" utility by how well its benefits generalize across four categories (learning conditions, student characteristics, materials, and criterion tasks). The two techniques with the strongest, most generalizable evidence are **practice testing** and **distributed practice**. Several popular student habits — especially rereading and highlighting — are rated **low** because durable-learning evidence is weak or inconsistent.

**Key facts.**
- **10 techniques rated** on a high/moderate/low scale (generalizability across 4 variable categories).
  - **HIGH (2):** Practice testing; distributed practice.
  - **MODERATE (3):** Elaborative interrogation; self-explanation; interleaved practice.
  - **LOW (5):** Summarization; highlighting/underlining; keyword mnemonic; imagery for text; rereading.
- Practice-testing benefit has **>100 years** of supporting evidence (traced to Abbott, 1909).
- Practitioner-article numbers (read from the full AFT PDF):
  - **84%** of students at an elite university reported rereading notes/text as a primary strategy (Karpicke et al., 2009) — heavy reliance on a *low*-utility method.
  - Self-explanation transfer experiment (Berry, 1983): self-explainers ≈ **90% vs <30%** on a logical-reasoning final test (~3×).
  - Interleaving (volume-of-solids problems; Rohrer & Taylor, 2007): interleavers ≈ **3×** the delayed-test accuracy of **blocked** practice.
- **Caveats.** Moderate techniques were downgraded for *insufficient* classroom evidence, not failure. Interleaving benefits **do not extend to all content** (e.g., no benefit for some vocabulary/grammar categories). Summarization needs substantial training. Even the best techniques only help if students are motivated to use them correctly.
- *Access:* AFT practitioner PDF read in full; Sage monograph abstract was 403-blocked, so its utility ratings were confirmed via secondary sources.

### Freeman et al. (2014) — *Active learning increases student performance in STEM*

**Citation.** Freeman, S., Eddy, S. L., McDonough, M., Smith, M. K., Okoroafor, N., Jordt, H., & Wenderoth, M. P. (2014). "Active learning increases student performance in science, engineering, and mathematics." *PNAS, 111*(23), 8410–8415.
**URL.** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4060654/

**Summary.** The largest meta-analysis of undergraduate STEM instruction to date, comparing traditional lecturing with active learning. Active learning raised exam/concept-inventory scores by just under half a standard deviation and substantially cut failure rates, with effects consistent across disciplines and class sizes and robust to publication-bias checks. The authors argue active learning should be the empirically validated default.

**Key facts.**
- **Design:** meta-analysis of **225 studies** in undergraduate STEM courses.
- **Exam performance:** weighted standardized mean difference **g = 0.47 SD** (n = 158 comparisons; P << 0.001) ≈ **+6% exam scores** / ~0.3 grade-points; moves a median student from the 50th to the **~68th percentile**.
- **Failure rates:** **33.8%** under lecturing vs **21.8%** under active learning — a **~55% higher** failure rate for lecturing. Odds ratio of failing under lecturing = **1.95** (n = 67 comparisons), ~**1.5×** risk ratio.
- **Robustness:** effects held across all STEM disciplines and class sizes (largest in classes ≤ 50); helped concept inventories more than course exams; fail-safe Ns = 114 (exams) / 438 (failure); no significant publication bias.
- **Caveats.** Instructors used active learning *voluntarily* (unknown if gains hold if mandated); failure-rate data were largely *quasi-random*, not fully randomized; the authors could **not** evaluate which *type/intensity* of active learning works best.
- *Access:* full text read (PMC open access).

### Deans for Impact (2015) / The Learning Scientists — practitioner summaries

**Citation.** Deans for Impact (2015). *The Science of Learning.* Austin, TX. · The Learning Scientists (Weinstein, Sumeracki & Caviglioli), *Six Strategies for Effective Learning.*
**URL.** https://www.learningscientists.org/ · Deans PDF: https://www.deansforimpact.org/files/assets/thescienceoflearning.pdf

**Summary.** Both are **practitioner translations of cognitive science, not original studies** (flagged as such). *The Science of Learning* organizes the evidence around six key questions, pairing each with cognitive principles and classroom implications. The Learning Scientists promote six evidence-based study strategies via free posters, slides, and a podcast.

**Key facts.**
- **Deans for Impact — six key questions (2015):** (1) How do students understand new ideas? (2) How do they learn & retain information? (3) How do they solve problems? (4) How does learning transfer? (5) What motivates students? (6) Common misconceptions about learning.
- **Misconceptions it debunks:** the "humans use only 10% of their brains" myth; **learning styles** / left-vs-right-brain; that novices and experts think alike; that memorization has no value.
- **The Learning Scientists — six strategies:** spaced practice, retrieval practice, elaboration, interleaving, concrete examples, dual coding.
- They explicitly note **uneven evidence** — "a lot of evidence supporting retrieval practice and spacing, whereas elaboration has more limitations" — and that the six are not exhaustive. Maps onto the peer-reviewed tutorial Weinstein, Madan & Sumeracki (2018), *Cognitive Research: Principles and Implications*.
- **Caveat.** Practitioner summaries, not empirical research; the currently hosted Deans PDF is a **2nd edition** that reorganizes the questions (adds "How do students self-regulate?").
- *Access:* Deans PDF read; Learning Scientists strategies confirmed via WebSearch (thin homepage content).

## 2. Retrieval practice & spacing

### Roediger & Karpicke (2006) — *The Power of Testing Memory*

**Citation.** Roediger, H. L., III, & Karpicke, J. D. (2006). "The Power of Testing Memory: Basic Research and Implications for Educational Practice." *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 1*(3), 181–210. (Reviews the companion experiment in *Psychological Science, 17*, 249–255, "Test-Enhanced Learning.")
**URL.** http://psychnet.wustl.edu/memory/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/Roediger-Karpicke-2006_PPS.pdf

**Summary.** A selective review arguing that retrieval practice (testing) directly boosts long-term retention beyond restudying for equal time — the "testing effect" — even without feedback. It walks through classic and the authors' own prose-passage experiments, then extends to classrooms and theory. The signature finding is that testing wins only at a *delay*; restudy can look better on an immediate test.

**Key facts.**
- **Headline prose-passage experiment** (study vs no-feedback test, then final test):
  - **5 min:** restudy **81%** > test **75%** (slight restudy edge immediately).
  - **2 days:** test **68%** > restudy **54%**.
  - **1 week:** test **56%** > restudy **42%** ← classic testing-beats-restudy-at-delay result.
- **Experiment 2 (SSSS vs SSST vs STTT):** at 5 min, recall tracked amount of *studying*; at 1 week, it tracked amount of *testing* (full reversal). **Proportional forgetting over 1 week: 52% (SSSS), 28% (SSST), 10% (STTT)** — more testing → less forgetting.
- Metacognitive twist: students *predicted* more studying would help most, despite it yielding the worst long-term retention.
- **Design/caveats.** Narrative/selective review (lab word-list & prose studies + some classroom), **not** a meta-analysis (no pooled effect size). Notes possible negative effects of testing (retrieval-induced forgetting, suggestibility) but treats them as generally small. Some figures are read from review charts (approximate).
- *Access:* full text read.

### Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted & Rohrer (2006) — distributed-practice meta-analysis

**Citation.** Cepeda, N. J., Pashler, H., Vul, E., Wixted, J. T., & Rohrer, D. (2006). "Distributed Practice in Verbal Recall Tasks: A Review and Quantitative Synthesis." *Psychological Bulletin, 132*(3), 354–380.
**URL.** http://uweb.cas.usf.edu/~drohrer/pdfs/Cepeda_et_al_2006PsychBull.pdf

**Summary.** A large quantitative meta-analysis of the spacing/lag effect in verbal recall, focused on the temporal variables (inter-study interval, ISI; retention interval, RI) earlier reviews ignored. It confirms a robust overall spacing benefit and shows ISI and RI act *jointly*: for any given RI there is a nonmonotonic (inverted-U) optimal ISI that **grows as the RI grows**.

**Key facts.**
- **Scope:** **839 assessments** across **317 experiments** in **184 articles** (screened from 427); yielded 169 effect sizes.
- **Overall spacing effect:** across **271 comparisons / 254 studies / 14,811 participants**, spaced recall **47.3%** vs massed **36.7%** (≈ **10.6 pp** absolute benefit; t(540) = 6.6, p < .001). Only **12 of 271** comparisons failed to show the effect (massing helped only at retention intervals of seconds).
- **Core finding:** optimal ISI rises with RI — at RI < 1 min, optimal ISI < 1 min; at RI ≥ 6 months, optimal ISI ≥ ~1 month. The optimal **ISI-to-RI ratio actually *decreases*** as RI grows (so the "10–20%" rule of thumb is approximate, strongest at shorter RIs).
- **Design/caveats.** Restricted to **verbal recall**. Reported mostly as accuracy differences, not Cohen's d (too few effect sizes). Between-bin comparisons confounded by task difficulty (authors add a within-study analysis); few primary studies used RIs ≥ 1 day.
- *Access:* full text read.

### Pashler, Rohrer, Cepeda & Carpenter (2007) — *Enhancing Learning and Retarding Forgetting*

**Citation.** Pashler, H., Rohrer, D., Cepeda, N. J., & Carpenter, S. K. (2007). "Enhancing Learning and Retarding Forgetting: Choices and Consequences." *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 14*(2), 187–193.
**URL.** https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/spaced-repetition/2007-pashler.pdf

**Summary.** An overview of the authors' research program on instructionally relevant "choices": spacing, feedback timing, retrieval practice, and guessing. Its flagship message is that for a given retention interval, memory is optimized when the inter-study gap is a modest fraction (**~10–20%**) of the final retention interval — and that too little spacing hurts more than too much. (This is the source of the "10–20%" rule cited in `LEARNING_SCIENCE.md`.)

**Key facts.**
- **Optimal-gap rule:** ISI ≈ **10–20% of the RI** (a rough band, *not* a fixed ratio — see caveat). Examples: 10-day RI → optimal ISI ≈ 1 day (≈0.1); 6-month RI → optimal ISI ≈ 28 days (≈0.15).
- **Web study (~1,800 subjects):** ISIs 20 min–15 weeks, RIs 1–50 weeks; 1-week RI → optimal ISI ≈ 1 day; 50-week RI → optimal ISI ≈ 3 weeks.
- **Asymmetry:** "too little spacing is worse than too much" — the curve falls off gently for over-long gaps, steeply for too-short gaps.
- **Feedback:** giving the correct answer after an *error* produced a **~5× increase** in 1-week recall; feedback after correct answers was largely unnecessary; **delayed feedback ≥ immediate** on a 2-week test.
- **Overlearning:** extra massed practice helped at 1 week but the benefit **vanished by 4 weeks** (inefficient).
- **Boundary:** **no spacing benefit** for perceptual categorization tasks — a limit on generality.
- **Design/caveats.** Narrative overview of the authors' own lab + web studies; the 10–20% figure is approximate and rests on a small number of long-RI experiments (several described as preliminary). The optimal ISI/RI ratio is **not constant** — Pashler's own web study (≈0.14 at a 1-week RI → ≈0.06 at a 50-week RI) and Cepeda 2006 both show it **declines as RI grows**, so the two example sets above should not be read as the ratio rising with RI.
- *Access:* full text read.

## 3. Interleaving (math-specific)

### Rohrer & Taylor (2007) — *The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning*

**Citation.** Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007). "The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning." *Instructional Science, 35*(6), 481–498.
**URL.** http://uweb.cas.usf.edu/~drohrer/pdfs/Rohrer%26Taylor2007IS.pdf

**Summary.** Two lab experiments with college students. Experiment 1 isolated *spacing* and *overlearning*; Experiment 2 isolated *interleaving* by having students compute the volumes of four obscure solids with practice that was either blocked by type or randomly mixed. On a one-week-later test, spaced practice strongly beat massed, overlearning showed no benefit, and interleaved practice vastly outperformed blocked — despite blocked practice looking better *during* practice (a classic "desirable difficulty" dissociation).

**Key facts.**
- **Experiment 2 (interleaving), 1-week delayed test:** mixed/interleaved **63%** (SE 12) vs blocked **20%** (SE 9); t(14) = 2.64, p < .05, **d = 1.34** (≈**3.1×** the blocked accuracy, +**215%**). N = **18** undergraduates.
- During practice the pattern reversed: blocked **89%** vs mixed **60%** (interleaving is harder in the moment). Nearly every test error was choosing the *wrong formula* → interleaving teaches *which* procedure to use (discrimination), not just *how*.
- **Experiment 1 (spacing/overlearning), 1-week test:** spacers **74%** (SE 8) > massers **49%** (SE 10) ≈ light massers **46%** (SE 7); F(2,57) = 3.59, p < .05. Overlearning (heavy vs light massing) showed **no** difference (p = .8). N = 66 (60 retained).
- **Design/caveats.** Laboratory, randomized, 1-week delay. Authors caution: college students only; the test mirrored practiced problems (no transfer measure); procedural (not conceptual) tasks; **very small N (18)** in Exp 2 (though effects were large).
- *Access:* full text read.

### Rohrer, Dedrick, Hartwig & Cheung (2020) — *A randomized controlled trial of interleaved mathematics practice*

**Citation.** Rohrer, D., Dedrick, R. F., Hartwig, M. K., & Cheung, C.-N. (2020). "A randomized controlled trial of interleaved mathematics practice." *Journal of Educational Psychology, 112*(1), 40–52.
**URL.** http://uweb.cas.usf.edu/~drohrer/pdfs/Rohrer_et_al_2020JEdPsych.pdf

**Summary.** A preregistered, cluster-randomized trial of interleaved math practice in real 7th-grade classrooms over ~5 months. Fifty-four classes did assignments whose critical problems were either interleaved or blocked; both groups then got the *same* interleaved review (equating test delay), followed ~1 month later by an unannounced test. Interleaving produced a large benefit, replicating small-scale lab findings in a naturalistic classroom setting.

**Key facts.**
- **Headline test scores:** interleaved **61%** (M = 60.7, SD 28.6) vs blocked **38%** (M = 37.6, SD 27.3).
- **Effect size: d = 0.83**, 95% CI [0.68, 0.97]; positive for **all 15 teachers** (ds 0.23–1.48). Multilevel model: interleaving = **+22.2 pp** (p < .001).
- **Design:** preregistered cluster RCT (OSF), within-teacher; **28 interleaved vs 26 blocked** classes.
- **Sample:** final **N = 787 students** (398 vs 389) across **15 teachers** and **5 schools**, honors 7th-grade math, 2017–2018. Test: 16 items, **blocked format** (chosen to work *against* interleaving), unannounced, α = .89, blind scoring. Baseline equivalence confirmed (grade-6 state scores nearly identical).
- **Pilot (1 yr earlier):** n = 83, interleaved 51% vs blocked 22%, **d = 0.97**.
- **Caveats.** Interleaving likely takes **more time per problem** (time-on-task not measured), so per-unit-time benefit may be smaller; benefit may shrink at shorter delays; may require *some* initial blocked practice and **corrective feedback**; it's really a "mostly interleaved vs mostly blocked" dose contrast.
- *Access:* full text read.

## 4. Mastery, worked examples, cognitive load

### Bloom (1984) — *The 2 Sigma Problem* (+ von Hippel 2024 critique)

**Citation.** Bloom, B. S. (1984). "The 2 Sigma Problem: The Search for Methods of Group Instruction as Effective as One-to-One Tutoring." *Educational Researcher, 13*(6), 4–16. · Critique: von Hippel, P. T. (2024). "Two-Sigma Tutoring: Separating Science Fiction from Science Fact." *Education Next, 24*(2).
**URL.** https://files.ascd.org/staticfiles/ascd/pdf/journals/ed_lead/el_198405_bloom.pdf · critique: https://www.educationnext.org/two-sigma-tutoring-separating-science-fiction-from-science-fact/

**Summary.** Bloom reported that students taught one-to-one with mastery-learning methods scored about **two standard deviations** higher than conventional-classroom students, while group-based mastery learning produced about **1 sigma**. Because 1:1 tutoring doesn't scale, he posed the "2 sigma problem": find group methods that approach the tutoring effect. The 2024 *Education Next* critique argues the headline is real but **inflated and never replicated** — measured on narrow experimenter-made tests, with the tutoring condition bundling extra testing/feedback and tutor training.

**Key facts.**
- **Core claims:** tutored student ≈ **2.0 SD** above the control mean (≈ **98th percentile**); mastery-learning student ≈ **1.0 SD** (≈ 84th). ~90% of tutored and ~70% of mastery students reached the level of the top 20% of conventional students. Time-on-task: 65% (conventional) → 75% (mastery) → 90%+ (tutoring).
- **Bloom's Table 1 effect sizes (sigma):** tutorial 1:1 = **2.00**; reinforcement = 1.20; feedback-corrective (mastery learning) = **1.00**; cues & explanations = 1.00; classroom participation = 1.00; time-on-task = 1.00; reading/study skills = 1.00; cooperative learning = 0.80; graded homework = 0.80; classroom morale = 0.60; initial cognitive prerequisites = 0.60; home-environment intervention = 0.50; peer/cross-age tutoring = 0.40; assigned homework = 0.30; higher-order questions = 0.30; new science/math curricula = 0.30; teacher expectancy = 0.30; peer-group influence = 0.20; advance organizers = 0.20; SES (contrast) = 0.25.
- **Evidence base:** two University of Chicago dissertations (Anania; Burke); ~3-week experiments with 4th/5th & 8th graders on *deliberately unfamiliar* topics.
- **Critique / caveats (von Hippel 2024, read in full):**
  - **Never replicated**; one dissertation never published.
  - **Narrow tests inflate effects:** Cohen, Kulik & Kulik (1982) found tutoring ≈ **0.84 SD** on narrow author-made tests vs **0.27 SD** on broad standardized tests; Kraft (2020): effects ~2× larger on narrow vs broad tests.
  - **The 2 sigma wasn't tutoring alone:** ~1.1 sigma is attributable to the extra testing + feedback the mastery group *also* got, leaving **~0.9 sigma** for tutoring (≈ the 0.84 narrow-test figure). Tutors were specially trained; tutoring *replaced* (not supplemented) class.
  - **Real-world tutoring is far below 2 sigma:** Cohen et al. avg ≈ **0.33 SD** (1 of 65 studies hit 2σ); Nickow, Oreopoulos & Quan (2020) avg ≈ **0.37 SD** (**0 of 96** studies hit 2σ). High-dosage programs ≈ 0.16–0.37 SD.
- *Access:* Bloom PDF is **scanned** — its Table 1 was machine-extracted and used as given; narrative confirmed via WebSearch. Critique read in full.

### Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler & Sweller (2003) — *The Expertise Reversal Effect*

**Citation.** Kalyuga, S., Ayres, P., Chandler, P., & Sweller, J. (2003). "The Expertise Reversal Effect." *Educational Psychologist, 38*(1), 23–31. (Companion review: Kalyuga, S. (2007), *Educational Psychology Review, 19*(4), 509–539.)
**URL.** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-007-9054-3

**Summary.** The expertise reversal effect is the finding that instructional techniques that help **novices** lose effectiveness and can **reverse** to become harmful as expertise grows: if Design A beats Design B for novices, B can beat A for experts. It is grounded in cognitive load theory — guidance substitutes for the schemas novices lack, but for experts the same guidance becomes redundant and imposes extraneous working-memory load.

**Key facts.**
- **Definition:** an aptitude–treatment interaction where guidance that benefits low-knowledge learners is redundant/detrimental for high-knowledge learners (effects first *disappear*, then *reverse*).
- **Mechanism (CLT):** working memory handles only "a few elements" at once; long-term-memory schemas act as single chunks and free capacity. Experts' internal schemas + external guidance → redundant cross-referencing → overload.
- **Worked-example reversal (Kalyuga et al., 2001):** novices learned more from worked examples (lower mental effort); with experience the advantage vanished and eventually **problem-solving beat studying** redundant examples → motivates the *guidance-fading effect*.
- **Other documented reversals:** split-attention→redundancy; modality→redundancy; isolated→interacting elements; imagination effect.
- **Effect sizes (secondary):** the 2003 paper is a qualitative review (no pooled d). Later work: Kalyuga (2007) reports the effect across ~26 studies (d ≈ 0.45–2.99); a 2025 meta-analysis (Endres et al.; 60 studies, 176 effects, 5,924 participants) found low-prior-knowledge learners do better with high assistance (**d = 0.505**) and high-prior-knowledge learners better with low assistance (**d = −0.428**); the effect is **asymmetric** (helping novices matters more than withholding help from experts).
- **Caveats.** 2003 paper reports no aggregate effect sizes; the numeric ds come from later reviews. Weaker for young children and humanities/language domains.
- *Access:* 2003 paper read via open mirror; Springer EPR 2007 page **timed out** → effect sizes via WebSearch.

### Sweller, Ayres & Kalyuga (2011) — *The Guidance Fading Effect*

**Citation.** Sweller, J., Ayres, P., & Kalyuga, S. (2011). "The Guidance Fading Effect." Ch. 13 in *Cognitive Load Theory* (pp. 171–182). Springer.
**URL.** https://cogscisci.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/08/sweller-guidance-fading.pdf

**Summary.** Because of the expertise reversal effect, instructional guidance should be **faded** as learners gain expertise. Worked examples are highly efficient for novices but become redundant — and impose extraneous load — for more knowledgeable learners, who then learn more from solving problems. The recommended mechanism is the **completion problem** (finish a partially worked solution), used to build a graded series of **faded worked examples** (fully worked → partially completed → full problem).

**Key facts.**
- **Worked-example effect:** for novices, studying worked examples beats solving equivalent problems — it removes heavy means–ends search and supports schema construction.
- **Completion-problem effect:** a problem + partial solution to finish bridges examples → full problems (van Merriënboer).
- **Guidance-fading effect:** gradually faded examples beat consistent worked examples, plain problems, or example–problem pairs. **Backward fading** (drop the last step first) vs **forward fading**.
- **CLT basis:** intrinsic + extraneous cognitive load and working-memory limits. **Fading only helps when intrinsic load is high** (hard material); easy material shows no fading benefit.
- **Representative findings (mostly directional):** Renkl et al. (2002) — backward fading beat example–problem pairs on a delayed near-transfer test, and was superior on far transfer; adding **self-explanation prompts** at faded steps boosted near + far transfer (Renkl & Atkinson 2001); **adaptive fading > fixed fading > problem solving** (Salden et al. 2010); rapid "first-step" diagnostics cut testing time **up to 5×**, enabling real-time adaptation.
- **Caveats.** Chapter is largely **qualitative** — few standardized effect sizes; adaptive-fading research is early-stage; benefits are expertise-dependent. (Note: this chapter does **not** state the "7±2"/"~4 elements" figures — those trace to Miller 1956 and are CLT background, not numbers from this chapter.)
- *Access:* full text read (clean conversion, ~36.8k chars).

## 5. Feedback, self-explanation, metacognition

### Hattie & Timperley (2007) — *The Power of Feedback*

**Citation.** Hattie, J., & Timperley, H. (2007). "The Power of Feedback." *Review of Educational Research, 77*(1), 81–112.
**URL.** https://blackburn.edu/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/99064620_the_power_of_feedback_-_hattie_and_timperley-1.pdf

**Summary.** A conceptual review (not a new meta-analysis) synthesizing prior meta-analyses to argue that feedback is among the most powerful influences on achievement — but that its effect is highly variable and can be **positive or negative**. It proposes a model where effective feedback answers three questions across four "levels," and concludes that feedback directed at the *self* (praise) is least effective, while feedback about *process* and *self-regulation* is most powerful.

**Key facts.**
- **Average effect of feedback ≈ d = 0.79** (synthesis of 12 meta-analyses, 196 studies, 6,972 effect sizes) — "twice the average" of all schooling influences (~0.40). (A broader table in the same paper lists feedback at **d = 0.95**; 0.79 is the most-cited figure.)
- **Three questions:** *Where am I going?* (feed up) · *How am I going?* (feed back) · *Where to next?* (feed forward).
- **Four levels:** task, **process**, **self-regulation**, self/praise. Verdict: "**FS [self/praise] is the least effective**; FR and FP are powerful for deep processing and mastery."
- **Feedback can harm:** Kluger & DeNisi (1996) meta-analysis (131 studies, 470 effects, 12,652 people): average **d = 0.38**, and **~38% of effects were negative**. Teacher-praise effect ≈ **0.12–0.14**.
- **Moderators (from the PDF tables):** feedback discouraging the student **−0.14** vs 0.33; task complexity not-complex 0.55 vs very-complex **0.03**; little threat to self-esteem 0.47 vs much threat **0.08**; difficult goals 0.51 vs "do your best" 0.30.
- **Caveats.** A synthesis-of-syntheses, not one experiment; underlying meta-analyses are "highly variable." Later re-analysis (Wisniewski et al. 2020) put the weighted mean lower (~0.48), so treat 0.79 as a synthesis figure, not a precise estimate.
- *Access:* assigned PDF scanned (tables machine-readable; used for moderator numbers); narrative verified against text-identical mirrors.

### Shute (2008) — *Focus on Formative Feedback*

**Citation.** Shute, V. J. (2008). "Focus on Formative Feedback." *Review of Educational Research, 78*(1), 153–189.
**URL.** https://myweb.fsu.edu/vshute/pdf/shute%202007_f.pdf

**Summary.** A narrative review cataloging feedback types and timing and the variables they interact with (learner ability, task complexity, motivation). Shute distills a "tangled, contradictory" literature into practical guidelines, concluding feedback helps only "if delivered correctly," and that the same feature can help or hurt depending on learner and task.

**Key facts.**
- **Formative feedback** = "information communicated to the learner intended to modify thinking or behavior to improve learning" — ideally specific, timely, nonevaluative, supportive, and task-focused.
- **Verification vs elaboration** (Kulhavy & Stock, 1989): verification = right/wrong; elaboration = the informational content guiding toward the correct answer. **Elaborated feedback generally beats verification alone.**
- **Do:** focus on the task not the learner; provide elaborated feedback in manageable units; be specific and clear; give feedback *after* an attempt.
- **Avoid:** normative comparisons; **grades alone** (Wiliam: grades-only = no gain, comments-only = large gains, grades+comments = no gain — students fixate on the grade); threatening self-esteem; **using praise** (diverts attention to the self).
- **Timing:** **immediate** feedback better for *difficult* tasks and for acquiring verbal/procedural/motor skills; **delayed** feedback better for *easy* tasks and associated with **better transfer**. Learner-ability rule: immediate + directive for low achievers; delayed + facilitative (hints) for high achievers.
- **Caveats.** Qualitative review (few pooled effect sizes); findings "inconsistent, contradictory, and highly variable"; prescriptions are heuristics.
- *Access:* full text read (~118k chars).

### Chi, Bassok, Lewis, Reimann & Glaser (1989) — *Self-Explanations*

**Citation.** Chi, M. T. H., Bassok, M., Lewis, M. W., Reimann, P., & Glaser, R. (1989). "Self-Explanations: How Students Study and Use Examples in Learning to Solve Problems." *Cognitive Science, 13*(2), 145–182.
**URL.** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1207/s15516709cog1302_1 (read via verbatim ERIC tech-report ED296291)

**Summary.** A fine-grained protocol-analysis study of how a small number of students studied three worked Newtonian-mechanics examples and then solved problems. Students who later solved well ("Good") spontaneously generated far more and better **self-explanations**, relating example steps to physics principles and accurately monitoring their own comprehension. This is the foundational paper on the "self-explanation effect" — but it is **observational/correlational**, not an experimental manipulation.

**Key facts.**
- **Small N = 8** students, split **post hoc by a median split** on problem-solving success into 4 "Good" and 4 "Poor."
- **⚠ The "82% vs 46%" figure (cited in `LEARNING_SCIENCE.md`):** these numbers *are* in Chi et al. (1989) — Good students' mean problem-solving success **82%** vs Poor **46%** — **but this is the success that *defines* the Good/Poor groups (the grouping criterion), NOT a self-explanation treatment-vs-control "post-test."** There was **no self-explanation intervention** here, so framing 82/46 as a self-explanation *effect* is a **misattribution**. The genuine finding in this paper is *correlational*. (The pretest/posttest self-explanation **prompting** study is a *different* paper — **Chi et al., 1994**, on the circulatory system.)
- **Self-explanation quantity:** Good produced ~**2.8×** more explanation *statements* (51 vs 18) and ~**6.8×** more raw protocol lines (142 vs 21) per example. Physics explanations: Good 15.9 vs Poor 4.3, and # of physics explanations **correlated r = .76** with problem-solving success.
- **Comprehension monitoring:** Good detected comprehension failures **9.3×** per example vs Poor **1.1**. Groups had **equal prior knowledge** and produced equal protocol lines while problem-solving (rules out "Good students just talk more").
- **Caveats.** Tiny N (8); groups defined post hoc by the outcome; physics-only; **correlational** — shows Good solvers self-explain more, not that prompting self-explanation *causes* gains.
- *Access:* full text read via identical ERIC technical-report version (Wiley page paywalled).

### Dunlosky & Rawson (2012) — *Overconfidence produces underachievement*

**Citation.** Dunlosky, J., & Rawson, K. A. (2012). "Overconfidence produces underachievement: Inaccurate self evaluations undermine students' learning and retention." *Learning and Instruction, 22*(4), 271–280.
**URL.** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475211000685

**Summary.** Two studies tested whether the **accuracy** of students' self-evaluations of learning is causally tied to long-term retention (the "accuracy-influences-memory," or AIM, hypothesis). Students self-paced study of key-term definitions, took practice cued-recall tests, judged their own correctness, repeated until self-judged correct three times, then took a final test two days later. The core finding: **overconfidence → premature termination of study → lower retention**, so greater self-evaluation accuracy predicts higher retention.

**Key facts.**
- **Design:** two studies, college students, key-term definitions; final cued-recall test **2 days later**. Study 1 *manipulated* judgment accuracy (idea-unit scoring standards vs none); Study 2 examined natural individual differences.
- **Main result:** in both studies, **greater monitoring accuracy → higher long-term retention**, holding after controlling for feedback, study effort, and trials-to-criterion.
- **Mechanism:** overconfident students stop studying before durable encoding — links overconfidence to weaker self-regulated learning.
- **⚠ Specific number (unverified):** a secondary summary reports the idea-unit-standard group retained **~17% more** than controls — **treat as approximate/unverified**; not confirmed against the paywalled primary text.
- **Caveats.** Narrow materials (key-term definitions, college students); the 17% figure is from a secondary source.
- *Access:* **secondary (WebSearch)** — primary article paywalled; design/conclusions confirmed via ERIC + abstract.

## 6. Representation, transfer, difficulty

### Mayer — *Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning* (CTML)

**Citation.** Mayer, R. E. (2005/2014). "Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning." In *The Cambridge Handbook of Multimedia Learning* (Ch. 3). Cambridge University Press.
**URL.** https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/cambridge-handbook-of-multimedia-learning/cognitive-theory-of-multimedia-learning/A49922ACB5BC6A37DDCCE4131AC217E5

**Summary.** CTML holds that messages designed in light of how the mind works produce more meaningful learning. It rests on three assumptions (dual channels, limited capacity, active processing) and specifies five processes (select words, select images, organize words, organize images, integrate). The foundational **multimedia principle**: people learn more deeply from words *and* pictures than words alone. A family of design principles then manages extraneous, essential, and generative processing.

**Key facts.**
- **Three assumptions:** dual channels (visual/verbal); limited capacity per channel; active processing.
- **Principle families:** reduce *extraneous* load (coherence, signaling, redundancy, spatial/temporal contiguity); manage *essential* load (segmenting, pre-training, modality); foster *generative* processing (personalization, voice, embodiment).
- **Representative median effect sizes (d) — from Mayer's own syntheses (secondary):** multimedia ≈ **1.67** (Mayer 2017); temporal contiguity ≈ 1.22–1.30; spatial contiguity ≈ 0.79–1.10; redundancy ≈ 0.86–0.87; coherence ≈ 0.70–0.90; modality ≈ 0.72–1.17; segmenting ≈ 0.70; signaling ≈ 0.41–0.74 (among the smallest); embodiment ≈ 0.36. Most principles fall **d ≈ 0.4–1.3**.
- **Caveats.** Medians across heterogeneous short lab studies (mostly novice learners); many principles have **boundary conditions** (typically stronger for low-knowledge learners — an expertise-reversal pattern); the same principle yields different ds across syntheses, so any single number is approximate.
- *Access:* assigned chapter abstract-only (paywalled); effect sizes are **secondary**, from Mayer & Moreno (2003), Cambridge Handbook Ch. 12, and Mayer (2017).

### Fyfe, McNeil, Son & Goldstone (2014) — *Concreteness Fading*

**Citation.** Fyfe, E. R., McNeil, N. M., Son, J. Y., & Goldstone, R. L. (2014). "Concreteness Fading in Mathematics and Science Instruction: A Systematic Review." *Educational Psychology Review, 26*(1), 9–25.
**URL.** https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1036777

**Summary.** Moves beyond the concrete-vs-abstract debate by recommending **concreteness fading**: begin with concrete materials, then *explicitly and gradually fade* toward abstract representations. The authors argue this progression captures benefits exceeding concrete-only or abstract-only materials, especially for **transfer** and generalization.

**Key facts.**
- **Definition:** a structured *concrete → abstract* progression (start embodied, gradually strip surface features, end with abstract symbols).
- **Four proposed benefits:** interpret opaque symbols via familiar objects; provide perceptual grounding; build memorable fallback images; guide learners to distill generalizable structure.
- **Transfer claim:** improves transfer beyond either extreme — "advantages that go beyond the sum."
- **Design/caveats.** A **systematic review / qualitative synthesis**, *not* a meta-analysis — **no pooled effect size**. Conclusions are partly theoretical; the *fading* (gradual, explicit transition) does the work — concrete materials alone can hinder transfer.
- *Access:* ERIC abstract only (full text behind Springer).

### Bjork & Bjork (2020) — *Desirable Difficulties in Theory and Practice*

**Citation.** Bjork, R. A., & Bjork, E. L. (2020). "Desirable difficulties in theory and practice." *Journal of Applied Research in Memory and Cognition, 9*(4), 475–479.
**URL.** https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2021/01/RABjorkELBjorkJARMAC2020ForPostingSingleSpaced.pdf

**Summary.** A reflective commentary recounting the origins of "desirable difficulties" in the Bjorks' **New Theory of Disuse**, which distinguishes **storage strength** from **retrieval strength**. Conditions that slow apparent learning and depress current performance often optimize long-term retention and transfer — so learners and teachers routinely misjudge learning. Difficulties are only *desirable* when learners can actually overcome them.

**Key facts.**
- **New Theory of Disuse:** each memory has *storage strength* (how well-learned) and *retrieval strength* (current accessibility). Storage strength doesn't decay; items become inaccessible, not lost.
- **Key interaction:** the higher current *retrieval* strength, the *smaller* the storage-strength gain from restudy; some forgetting yields *larger* gains — so **forgetting can enhance learning** (why spacing/variation help).
- **Desirable difficulties:** spacing, interleaving, retrieval practice/testing, varying practice conditions, generation, reducing/spacing feedback.
- **Performance ≠ learning:** fast performance gains often fail to support long-term retention; current performance is an unreliable index of learning.
- **Illustration:** Florida International University's law school reportedly rose to **1st** in state bar passage after incorporating desirable difficulties (cited from Schulze 2020).
- **Caveats.** *Desirable* is essential — a difficulty helps only if the learner can overcome it (else it's "undesirable"); optimal difficulty depends on prior knowledge (Guadagnoli & Lee's Challenge Point). Theoretical commentary — **no effect sizes**.
- *Access:* full text read.

### Csikszentmihalyi (1990) — *Flow*

**Citation.** Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1990). *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience.* New York: Harper & Row (excerpt).
**URL.** https://files.blogs.baruch.cuny.edu/wp-content/blogs.dir/2418/files/2013/04/Mihaly-Csikszentmihalyi-Flow.pdf

**Summary.** Flow is the "optimal experience" — complete, effortless absorption in an intrinsically rewarding activity. Across cultures and activities, people describe enjoyment in near-identical terms (eight components). Flow occurs at the **balance point between anxiety and boredom**, when perceived challenge matches perceived skill; since skills grow, staying in flow requires progressively higher challenges, driving growth.

**Key facts.**
- **Challenge–skill balance ("flow channel"):** enjoyment appears "at the boundary between boredom and anxiety." Challenge **>** skill → **anxiety**; skill **>** challenge → **boredom**; flow lies between. What matters is the *perceived* challenge/skill.
- **Dynamic growth:** a learner in flow eventually drifts to boredom (skills outpace challenge) or anxiety (challenge outpaces skill); returning to flow requires raising challenge or building skill — re-entering at higher complexity.
- **Eight components/conditions:** (1) completable, challenge–skill-matched task; (2) deep concentration; (3) **clear goals**; (4) **immediate feedback**; (5) merging of action & awareness; (6) sense of control; (7) loss of self-consciousness; (8) altered sense of time.
- **Autotelic experience:** an activity done as an end in itself (intrinsically rewarding); external incentives may be needed for the first steps.
- **Caveats.** Theory grounded in interview/Experience-Sampling data — **not an RCT**, no effect sizes; descriptive/phenomenological; flow depends on individual perception.
- *Access:* full text read (relevant chapters of the book PDF).

## 7. Motivation & engagement

### Ryan & Deci (2000) — *Self-Determination Theory*

**Citation.** Ryan, R. M., & Deci, E. L. (2000). "Self-Determination Theory and the Facilitation of Intrinsic Motivation, Social Development, and Well-Being." *American Psychologist, 55*(1), 68–78.
**URL.** https://www.selfdeterminationtheory.org/SDT/documents/2000_RyanDeci_SDT.pdf

**Summary.** A foundational theory/review (no new data) presenting Self-Determination Theory. Humans have three innate psychological needs whose satisfaction underlies autonomous motivation, well-being, and healthy development; thwarting them diminishes motivation and well-being. It synthesizes two sub-theories — Cognitive Evaluation Theory (what enhances/undermines intrinsic motivation) and Organismic Integration Theory (a continuum of extrinsic motivation by degree of internalization).

**Key facts.**
- **Three basic needs:** **autonomy, competence, relatedness** — innate, essential, universal.
- **Intrinsic vs extrinsic:** intrinsic = doing an activity for its inherent satisfaction; extrinsic = for a separable outcome (but extrinsic motivation varies in autonomy).
- **Internalization continuum:** amotivation → **external** → **introjected** → **identified** → **integrated** regulation → intrinsic motivation.
- **Rewards undermine intrinsic motivation:** cites the **Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999)** meta-analysis — **expected tangible rewards contingent on performance reliably undermine intrinsic motivation** by shifting the perceived locus of causality to external. Threats, deadlines, and pressured evaluations do likewise; choice and positive competence feedback enhance it.
- **Caveats.** Theory/review — reports **no original effect sizes**; cited numbers belong to other works.
- *Access:* full text read (assigned PDF scanned; full text obtained via publisher's text).

### Lepper, Greene & Nisbett (1973) — the overjustification effect

**Citation.** Lepper, M. R., Greene, D., & Nisbett, R. E. (1973). "Undermining children's intrinsic interest with extrinsic reward: A test of the 'overjustification' hypothesis." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 28*(1), 129–137.
**URL.** https://bingschool.stanford.edu/sites/bingschool/files/1973_lepperetal.pdf

**Summary.** A classic **field experiment** testing whether inducing a child to do an intrinsically interesting activity *as a means to a reward* undermines later intrinsic interest. Preschoolers who already liked drawing with markers were assigned to expected-reward, unexpected-reward, or no-reward conditions, then covertly observed 1–2 weeks later. As predicted, children who drew *to get* a reward later chose the activity about **half as much** as the others.

**Key facts.**
- **Design:** between-subjects field experiment; children pre-selected for baseline interest, blocked and randomly assigned; outcome observed unobtrusively **7–14 days** later. Bing Nursery School, Stanford; ages ~3–5.
- **Conditions:** (1) expected award ("Good Player" certificate); (2) unexpected award (same reward, no foreknowledge); (3) no award.
- **Sample:** final **N = 51** (expected 18, unexpected 18, no-award 15).
- **Primary result — % of free-choice time on the target activity:** expected **8.59%** vs no-award **16.73%** vs unexpected **18.09%** — the expected-reward group played **~half as much** (≈47–51% of the others). ANOVA F = 3.25 (df 2/48, p < .05); planned contrast F = 6.19 (p < .025). Reward also lowered drawing *quality*.
- **Caveats (authors').** Conditions deliberately stacked (attractive activity, high initial interest, superfluous reward); warns against overgeneralizing to settings with low initial interest or where rewards are needed to elicit behavior; small N; symbolic (not monetary) reward.
- *Access:* full text read (assigned PDF scanned — tables extracted directly; text via mirror).

### Sisk et al. (2018) — growth mindset, two meta-analyses

**Citation.** Sisk, V. F., Burgoyne, A. P., Sun, J., Butler, J. L., & Macnamara, B. N. (2018). "To What Extent and Under Which Circumstances Are Growth Mind-Sets Important to Academic Achievement? Two Meta-Analyses." *Psychological Science, 29*(4), 549–571.
**URL.** https://englelab.gatech.edu/articles/2018/Sisk,%20Burgoyne%20et%20al.%20(2018)%20-%20Mindset%20and%20Academic%20Achievement.pdf

**Summary.** Two PRISMA-compliant random-effects meta-analyses: one on the correlation between students' naturally held mindsets and achievement, one on the effectiveness of growth-mindset *interventions*. Both overall effects were **weak** with very high heterogeneity — but a few subgroups (notably low-SES students) showed meaningfully larger intervention benefits, partly supporting targeted versions of the theory.

**Key facts.**
- **Meta-analysis 1 (correlation):** **k = 273 effects, N = 365,915** students; **overall r = .10** [.08, .13]. **Heterogeneity I² = 96.3%** — 58% of effects not different from zero, 6% significantly negative, 37% positive. Risk status and SES were *not* significant moderators of the correlation.
- **Meta-analysis 2 (interventions):** **k = 43, N = 57,155**; **overall d = 0.08** [0.02, 0.14], p = .010 (small).
  - **Low-SES students: d = 0.34** [0.07, 0.62] (SES a significant moderator); middle/upper d = 0.03 (n.s.).
  - High-academic-risk subgroup d ≈ 0.17 (mostly n.s.). Interactive interventions d = 0.09; passive d = 0.02 (n.s.).
- **Caveats.** Overall effects "weak"; massive heterogeneity; benefits concentrated in narrow subgroups resting on few effect sizes (low-SES = 7); ~46% of studies with manipulation checks failed to shift mindset; larger effects when no manipulation check was used (hints at fragility/inflation).
- *Access:* full text read.

### Sailer & Homner (2019) — *The Gamification of Learning: a Meta-analysis*

**Citation.** Sailer, M., & Homner, L. (2020). "The Gamification of Learning: a Meta-analysis." *Educational Psychology Review, 32*, 77–112. (Online 2019.)
**URL.** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-019-09498-w

**Summary.** A random-effects meta-analysis of gamification's effects on three outcome types versus conventional instruction. It finds significant **small** positive effects across cognitive, motivational, and behavioral outcomes, with **high heterogeneity** — so it tests *how* to gamify. Game fiction and combining competition with collaboration mattered most for behavioral outcomes.

**Key facts.**
- **Sample:** 5,548 records screened → **38 publications / 40 experiments**; effect size = Hedges' g.
- **Three summary effects:** **cognitive g = 0.49** [0.30, 0.69] (k = 19, N = 1,686); **motivational g = 0.36** [0.18, 0.54] (k = 16, N = 2,246); **behavioral g = 0.25** [0.04, 0.46] (k = 9–10, N = 951).
- **High heterogeneity:** I² ≈ 72% / 75% / 64% (all p < .01).
- **Design matters:** game fiction and social interaction were significant moderators **for behavioral outcomes only**; **competition + collaboration** beat no social interaction. In high-rigor subsets the cognitive effect stayed stable but motivational/behavioral effects were less stable.
- **Caveats.** Effects small-to-moderate at best; large unexplained heterogeneity; field criticized for weak rigor; possible novelty effects; gamification augments (not replaces) good instruction.
- *Access:* full text read (open access).

## 8. How leading apps implement this

### Settles & Meeder (2016) — Duolingo half-life regression

**Citation.** Settles, B., & Meeder, B. (2016). "A Trainable Spaced Repetition Model for Language Learning." *Proceedings of ACL 2016*, 1848–1858.
**URL.** https://research.duolingo.com/papers/settles.acl16.pdf · code: https://github.com/duolingo/halflife-regression

**Summary.** Introduces **Half-Life Regression (HLR)**, a trainable spaced-repetition model marrying Ebbinghaus-style forgetting curves with machine learning fit to large-scale Duolingo logs (instead of hand-picked constants). HLR estimates the "half-life" (memory strength) of each student–word pair and predicts recall probability; the classic Pimsleur and Leitner schedules are shown to be special cases. It beats those baselines on historical data and lifted engagement when deployed.

**Key facts.**
- **Core model:** recall probability **p = 2^(−Δ/h)** (Δ = time since last practice, h = half-life); estimated half-life grows exponentially with successful exposures.
- **Accuracy:** HLR **reduced error ~45%+** vs baselines — MAE: HLR **0.128** vs Leitner 0.235, Pimsleur 0.445, logistic regression 0.211.
- **Engagement:** **+12%** in daily activity in a production A/B test (specifically the HLR-lex vs HLR experiment, 3.3M students).
- **Dataset (⚠ correction to a common figure):** **12.9 million** practice-session traces (two weeks of logs), 90/10 train/test — *not* "hundreds of millions."
- **Caveats.** Lexeme-tag features barely helped and caused **overfitting** + cold-start, so Duolingo deployed the simpler **HLR-lex** (interaction features only); absolute AUC is low (~0.51–0.54) due to a noisy task; the ~5% MoM user-growth attribution is an estimate, not a controlled measure.
- *Access:* full text read.

### Anki — SM-2 vs FSRS scheduling

**Citation.** "What spaced repetition algorithm does Anki use?" — Anki FAQs (AnkiWeb).
**URL.** https://faqs.ankiweb.net/what-spaced-repetition-algorithm · FSRS project: https://github.com/open-spaced-repetition

**Summary.** As of Anki 23.10 the app ships two schedulers: a customized variant of **SuperMemo SM-2** (the long-time default) and **FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler)**. FSRS models memory with the three-component **DSR** model and uses machine learning on a user's own review history to fit per-user parameters, letting users target a chosen retention level.

**Key facts.**
- **SM-2 variant:** 4 answer buttons; default ease factor **2.5 (250%)**, floor 130%; intervals scale by ease.
- **FSRS = DSR model:** **Difficulty** (item complexity), **Stability** (days for recall probability to fall from 100% → **90%**), **Retrievability** (current recall probability). Stability/Difficulty change only on review; Retrievability changes daily. ML-fit per user (users told not to hand-tune).
- **Desired retention:** users set a target to balance recall vs workload ("higher retention → more reviews/day"). FSRS needs **fewer reviews** for the same retention than the default algorithm. Anki uses FSRS (not SuperMemo's newest) because the latter is **proprietary**; "preliminary tests... FSRS is roughly on par with SM-17."
- **⚠ Caveats on numbers.** This FAQ page does **not** print the **90% default-retention** figure (widely true of Anki, but not from this page) and gives **no percentage** for the "fewer reviews" claim — don't attribute a specific % to this source.
- *Access:* full text read.

### Khan Academy — Mastery system & efficacy

### Brilliant — learn-by-doing pedagogy

**Citation.** "Game on: Solving for x-citement with interactive Algebra," Brilliant Blog (*Solving Equations* course).
**URL.** https://blog.brilliant.org/solving-equations/

**Summary.** A company blog post laying out Brilliant's **learn-by-doing** pedagogy: lean on the learner's intuition, drop them into interactive problems *before* introducing terminology, and build confidence through hands-on action — "like a great mobile game."

**Key facts.**
- **"Action first. Theory later.":** learners are dropped "right into the action" via drag-and-drop equation building and manipulatives; **only after** intuitively learning a technique does Brilliant "introduce the algebraic names" — confirming the "name the technique afterward" design.
- The first lesson uses puzzles solvable **by intuition alone**, to show "you already know algebra"; later lessons build a progressively harder "arsenal."
- **⚠ Caveat.** This is a **marketing/pedagogy blog post, not a study** — **no metrics, sample sizes, or efficacy data**. "Immediate feedback" / "bite-sized lessons" are *consistent with* the framing but not stated verbatim here.

## Master fact sheet

A single deduplicated, scannable list of the most usable numbers. Each bullet ends
with the short source tag; see the matching section for full context and caveats.

**Active learning & "what works"**
- Active learning raises STEM exam scores **~0.47 SD** (~+6%, 50th→68th pctile) and cuts failure rates from **34% → 22%** (~55% more failures under lecturing; OR 1.95); meta-analysis of **225 studies**. *(Freeman 2014)*
- The two highest-utility study techniques are **practice testing** and **distributed practice**; rereading & highlighting rate **low**. *(Dunlosky 2013)*
- **84%** of elite-university students rely on rereading — a low-utility strategy. *(Dunlosky 2013)*
- Practitioner consensus "6 strategies": spacing, retrieval, elaboration, interleaving, concrete examples, dual coding — but evidence is uneven (strongest for retrieval & spacing). *(Learning Scientists)*

**Retrieval practice (testing effect)**
- Testing beats restudy at a delay: **56% vs 42%** at 1 week (and 68% vs 54% at 2 days); restudy only wins immediately (81% vs 75% at 5 min). *(Roediger & Karpicke 2006)*
- More testing → less forgetting: 1-week forgetting of **52% (study-only) vs 10% (test-heavy)**. *(Roediger & Karpicke 2006)*
- Giving the correct answer after an error → **~5× more** 1-week recall. *(Pashler 2007)*

**Spacing**
- Spaced practice beats massed: **47.3% vs 36.7%** (~**10.6 pp**) across 254 studies / **14,811** participants; only 12 of 271 comparisons failed. *(Cepeda 2006)*
- Optimal study gap ≈ **10–20% of the retention interval** (e.g., 1-week RI → ~1-day gap; 6-month RI → ~1-month gap); too little spacing is worse than too much. *(Pashler 2007)* — note the optimal *ratio* actually shrinks as RI grows. *(Cepeda 2006)*
- Overlearning (extra massed practice) is inefficient — Pashler found a 1-week benefit that **vanishes by 4 weeks**, while Rohrer & Taylor found **no benefit even at 1 week** (heavy vs. light massing, p = .8). *(Pashler 2007; Rohrer & Taylor 2007)*

**Interleaving (math)**
- Lab: interleaved **63% vs 20%** blocked on a 1-week test (**d = 1.34**), despite blocked looking better during practice (89% vs 60%); N = 18. *(Rohrer & Taylor 2007)*
- Classroom RCT: interleaved **61% vs 38%** blocked (**d = 0.83**), ~1-month delay, **N = 787** 7th-graders, positive for all 15 teachers. *(Rohrer 2020)*
- Most interleaving gains come from learning *which* strategy to use (discrimination), not *how*. *(Rohrer & Taylor 2007)*

**Mastery & cognitive load**
- Bloom: 1:1 tutoring ≈ **2 SD** (98th pctile), mastery learning ≈ **1 SD** — but **never replicated**; realistic tutoring averages **~0.33–0.37 SD** (0 of 96 modern studies hit 2σ), and narrow author-made tests inflate effects ~2×. *(Bloom 1984; von Hippel 2024)*
- Mastery-learning feedback-corrective effect ≈ **1.0 sigma** in Bloom's table. *(Bloom 1984)*
- **Expertise reversal:** guidance that helps novices can *hurt* experts; meta-analytic ds ≈ **+0.51** (novices w/ high assistance) vs **−0.43** (experts w/ low assistance); asymmetric. *(Kalyuga; Endres 2025)*
- **Fade guidance** worked → completion → full problems; **adaptive fading > fixed fading > problem solving**; only helps when material is hard (high intrinsic load). *(Sweller 2011)*

**Feedback & self-explanation**
- Feedback's average effect ≈ **d = 0.79** (~2× the average schooling influence) — but **~38% of feedback effects are negative** (Kluger & DeNisi). *(Hattie & Timperley 2007)*
- Feedback about the **self/praise is least effective** (~0.12); process & self-regulation feedback are most powerful. *(Hattie & Timperley 2007)*
- **Elaborated** feedback beats right/wrong verification; **grades alone = no gain, comments alone = large gains**; praise diverts attention to the self. *(Shute 2008)*
- Timing: **immediate** feedback for hard tasks / low achievers; **delayed** feedback aids transfer / high achievers. *(Shute 2008; Pashler 2007)*
- Self-explanation: better solvers generate ~**2.8× more** explanations (r = .76 with success) — but this is **correlational**; the "**82% vs 46%**" figure is the group-defining success criterion, **not** a self-explanation intervention result (that's Chi et al. 1994). *(Chi 1989)*
- **Overconfidence → premature study termination → lower retention**; accurate self-evaluation predicts better retention (the ~17% gain figure is unverified). *(Dunlosky & Rawson 2012)*

**Representation, transfer & difficulty**
- Multimedia design principles: most effects **d ≈ 0.4–1.3** (multimedia ~1.67; contiguity ~0.8–1.3; signaling among smallest ~0.4); stronger for low-knowledge learners. *(Mayer)*
- **Concreteness fading** (concrete → abstract) improves transfer beyond concrete-only or abstract-only; qualitative review (no pooled d). *(Fyfe 2014)*
- **Desirable difficulties** (spacing, interleaving, testing, varied/generative practice) depress short-term performance but improve long-term retention — *only if* the learner can overcome them. *(Bjork & Bjork 2020)*
- **Flow** occurs when perceived challenge ≈ perceived skill; needs clear goals + immediate feedback; challenge > skill → anxiety, skill > challenge → boredom. *(Csikszentmihalyi 1990)*

**Motivation & engagement**
- Three innate needs: **autonomy, competence, relatedness**; supporting them fosters intrinsic motivation. *(Ryan & Deci 2000)*
- **Expected tangible rewards reliably undermine intrinsic motivation** (Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999 meta-analysis). *(Ryan & Deci 2000)*
- Overjustification: preschoolers rewarded for drawing later did it **~half as much** (8.6% vs ~17–18% of free time); keep rewards informational, not controlling. *(Lepper 1973)*
- Growth-mindset effects are **small**: overall correlation **r = .10**; intervention **d = 0.08** (but **d = 0.34** for low-SES students); huge heterogeneity — adopt cheaply, don't over-claim. *(Sisk 2018)*
- Gamification effects are small-to-moderate: **cognitive g = 0.49, motivational g = 0.36, behavioral g = 0.25**, high heterogeneity; implementation matters (game fiction, competition+collaboration). *(Sailer & Homner 2019)*

**How leading apps do it**
- Duolingo HLR: recall **p = 2^(−Δ/h)**; **~45%+** lower error than baselines; **+12%** daily engagement A/B lift; trained on **12.9M** traces. *(Settles & Meeder 2016)*
- Anki FSRS (**D**ifficulty/**S**tability/**R**etrievability): stability = days for recall to fall to **90%**; targets a user-set desired retention; fewer reviews for the same retention. *(Anki)*
- Khan Academy ladder: **Familiar → Proficient → Mastered** (50/80/100 pts); mastery can be **demoted two levels** if a later score < 70%; "skills to proficient+" correlates with MAP Growth (correlational). *(Khan Academy)*
- Brilliant: **action before theory** — drop into interactive problems, name the technique afterward. *(Brilliant)*

## Contradictions & tensions across papers

This section catalogs places where the sources above **genuinely disagree** (or are
routinely read as disagreeing) — as distinct from the within-source data-integrity
corrections in [Access & limitations](#access--limitations). Most resolve once you
separate **short-term performance from long-term learning**, or account for
**different populations, tasks, and conditions** — but each should be applied with
the conflict in view.

- **Extrinsic rewards — undermining vs. effective.**
  - *Disagreement:* Lepper et al. (1973) and Ryan & Deci (2000, via Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999) find **expected tangible rewards reliably undermine intrinsic motivation**, yet Sailer & Homner (2019) find **gamification improves outcomes** (cognitive g = 0.49), and the §8 apps (Khan Mastery Points; Brilliant's "great mobile game" framing) are built on those very reward structures.
  - *Reconciliation:* overjustification is specific to **expected, controlling, performance-contingent** rewards on **already-interesting** tasks. **Informational** rewards (signaling competence/progress) and rewards that bootstrap a **low-initial-interest** activity are consistent with SDT. → Keep reward systems informational, not controlling.

- **Flow (challenge ≈ skill) vs. desirable difficulties.**
  - *Disagreement:* Csikszentmihalyi (1990) locates the optimum where **challenge matches skill** (challenge > skill → anxiety); Bjork & Bjork (2020) deliberately **add difficulty that depresses current performance** to boost retention.
  - *Reconciliation:* they optimize different axes — flow targets **momentary engagement**, desirable difficulties target **durable learning** ("performance ≠ learning"). Both require the difficulty to be **surmountable**: Bjork's "*desirable*" sits at the upper edge of the flow channel (cf. Guadagnoli & Lee's Challenge Point).

- **Feedback timing — immediate vs. delayed.**
  - *Disagreement:* Flow (immediate feedback as a condition) and Brilliant favor **immediate** feedback, and Shute (2008) recommends it for hard tasks / low achievers; but Pashler (2007) finds **delayed ≥ immediate** at 2 weeks, Shute finds **delayed aids transfer** / high achievers, and Bjork lists **reducing/spacing feedback** as a desirable difficulty — in tension with Hattie & Timperley's "feedback is among the most powerful influences."
  - *Reconciliation:* timing interacts with **task difficulty, learner level, and outcome measured** — immediate aids engagement and acquisition; delayed/spaced can aid transfer and long-term retention. Not a true contradiction once the moderators are specified.

- **Overlearning at a 1-week delay — benefit vs. none.**
  - *Disagreement:* Pashler (2007) reports overlearning **helps at 1 week** (gone by 4 weeks); Rohrer & Taylor (2007, Exp 1) find **no benefit even at 1 week** (heavy vs. light massing, p = .8).
  - *Reconciliation:* both conclude overlearning is **inefficient** long-term; the short-term discrepancy likely reflects materials/dose. → Treat overlearning as low-value regardless.

- **The spacing ratio — fixed "10–20%" vs. shrinking with RI.**
  - *Disagreement:* Pashler (2007) popularizes a **~10–20% ISI/RI rule of thumb**; Cepeda (2006) — and Pashler's *own* web study — show the optimal ratio **declines as the retention interval grows** (≈0.14 at a 1-week RI → ≈0.06 at 50 weeks).
  - *Reconciliation:* "10–20%" is a coarse band centered on short-to-medium RIs; for long RIs the optimal *fraction* is smaller (though the absolute gap keeps growing). → Use the rule as a starting heuristic, not a constant.

- **Interleaving — "moderate" rating vs. large effects.**
  - *Disagreement:* Dunlosky et al. (2013) rate interleaving **moderate** utility; Rohrer & Taylor (2007, d = 1.34) and Rohrer et al. (2020, d = 0.83) report **large** benefits.
  - *Reconciliation:* mostly chronology and scope — the 2013 rating reflected **thin classroom evidence at the time** (and interleaving doesn't help all content types); the 2020 classroom RCT later supplied that evidence. → Not a real dispute about magnitude where interleaving applies.

> **Note.** These are cross-source *tensions*, not errors in any single paper. Famous
> single-source corrections (Bloom's unreplicated 2σ; the Chi 1989 "82% vs 46%"
> misattribution; feedback's d = 0.79 vs 0.48; Duolingo's 12.9M traces) are tracked
> separately under [Access & limitations](#access--limitations).

**Data-integrity flags (commonly repeated but corrected here):**
- The "**82% vs 46% post-test**" attributed to **Chi et al. 1989** is the *grouping criterion* (Good vs Poor solver success), **not** a self-explanation intervention outcome; the controlled self-explanation prompting study is **Chi et al. 1994**.
- **Bloom's 2 sigma** was never replicated and is inflated by narrow tests and bundled testing/feedback (real-world tutoring ≈ 0.3–0.4 SD).
- **Duolingo's** training set is **12.9M** traces (not "hundreds of millions"); the **+12%** engagement lift is the HLR-lex vs HLR experiment.
- The **Anki** FAQ page does not itself state the **90%** default retention or a "% fewer reviews" figure — don't cite those numbers to that page.
